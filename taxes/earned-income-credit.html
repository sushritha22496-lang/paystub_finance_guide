Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) 2024: Amounts & Eligibility

📅 Updated 2024⏱ 6 min read

The Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) is one of the largest anti-poverty tax programs in the US—a fully refundable credit worth up to $7,830 in 2024 for workers with 3 or more children. Even workers without children can claim up to $632. About 1 in 5 eligible workers fails to claim it every year.

2024 EITC Maximum Credit Amounts

⚠️
Investment income limit: $11,600

If your investment income (interest, dividends, capital gains, rental income) exceeds $11,600 in 2024, you cannot claim the EITC—regardless of your earned income level.

How the EITC Is Calculated

The EITC has three zones: it phases in (grows) as earned income rises, plateaus at the maximum credit, then phases out as income continues to rise:

  • Phase-in: Credit grows by a percentage of each dollar earned (e.g., 34% for 1-child families)
  • Plateau: Credit stays at maximum within a range
  • Phase-out: Credit reduces by ~15–21 cents per dollar of income above threshold

The credit is based on the lower of earned income or AGI. Use IRS EITC Assistant at IRS.gov to calculate your exact credit.

Qualifying Child Requirements

  • Relationship: Son, daughter, stepchild, foster child, sibling, or descendant
  • Age: Under 19, or under 24 if a full-time student, or any age if permanently disabled
  • Residency: Lived with you in the US for more than half the year
  • SSN: Valid Social Security Number required
  • Not claimed by another filer — tiebreaker rules apply if multiple people could claim the same child

EITC Without Children (2024)

Workers without children can claim the EITC if they are ages 25–64, are not a dependent of another taxpayer, and lived in the US more than half the year. The maximum is $632 with an income limit of $18,591 (single) or $25,511 (MFJ).

💡
Prior year lookback rule

If your 2024 earned income is lower than your 2023 earned income, you can use your 2023 earned income to calculate your 2024 EITC (and CTC). This helps workers who had a lower-income year due to layoffs, medical leave, or other setbacks.

Self-Employment Tax: Rate, Deduction & How to Pay (2024)

📅 Updated 2024⏱ 6 min read

Self-employment tax is 15.3%—the combined employee and employer share of Social Security (12.4%) and Medicare (2.9%). Employees only pay half because employers cover the other half. Freelancers, independent contractors, and sole proprietors pay both halves. The good news: you can deduct half of SE tax from your income before calculating income tax.

How Self-Employment Tax Is Calculated

SE tax applies to 92.35% of net self-employment income (a proxy for the employee share—since employees pay FICA on gross, self-employed pay on net minus the employer's "share"):

StepCalculationExample ($80,000 net profit)
1. Net SE incomeRevenue − business expenses$80,000
2. SE income subject to taxNet income × 92.35%$73,880
3. Social Security portion (12.4%)× 12.4% (up to $168,600 SS wage base)$9,161.12
4. Medicare portion (2.9%)× 2.9% (no cap)$2,142.52
5. Total SE TaxSum of SS + Medicare$11,303.64
6. Deductible halfSE Tax ÷ 2$5,651.82 (reduces AGI)

The Half-SE-Tax Deduction

You can deduct 50% of your self-employment tax as an above-the-line deduction on Schedule 1 (Form 1040). This reduces your adjusted gross income (AGI) and therefore your federal income tax—but it does not reduce the SE tax itself.

In the example above: deducting $5,651.82 at a 22% income tax rate saves $1,243 in income tax.

Paying SE Tax: Quarterly Estimated Payments

Since no employer withholds taxes for you, you must make quarterly estimated payments (or face underpayment penalties):

  • Q1 (Jan–Mar): Due April 15
  • Q2 (Apr–May): Due June 17
  • Q3 (Jun–Aug): Due September 16
  • Q4 (Sep–Dec): Due January 15 of following year

Pay via IRS Direct Pay, EFTPS, or when filing. The safe harbor rule: pay at least 100% of last year's tax (110% if AGI over $150,000) to avoid penalties.

Strategies to Reduce SE Tax

  • S-Corp election — salary yourself a "reasonable" wage (subject to FICA) and take remaining profit as distributions (not subject to SE tax); saves 15.3% on distributions above salary
  • Solo 401(k) — contribute up to $69,000/year (2024) pre-tax; reduces net income subject to SE tax
  • SEP-IRA — contribute up to 25% of net earnings ($69,000 max); reduces taxable income and SE income
  • Deduct all business expenses — home office, equipment, vehicle, health insurance premiums all reduce net profit subject to SE tax
⚠️
SE tax kicks in at $400 of net income

You must pay self-employment tax if your net self-employment income is $400 or more. This is a common surprise for people with side gigs who assume they don't earn enough to owe SE tax.

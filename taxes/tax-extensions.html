Tax Extensions: How to Get 6 More Months to File

Updated June 2026 • 8 min read
Quick Answer: Filing Form 4868 by April 15 gives you until October 15 to submit your return — but you must still pay any taxes owed by April 15 to avoid interest and penalties.

What a Tax Extension Actually Does

A tax extension gives you extra time to file your return — it does not give you extra time to pay any taxes you owe. This distinction trips up millions of filers every year.

With an extension, your new filing deadline moves from April 15 to October 15. No explanation or approval is required — it is an automatic 6-month extension for anyone who files on time.

How to File Form 4868

There are several ways to request an extension:

  • IRS Free File: File Form 4868 electronically for free at IRS.gov, even if your income exceeds the Free File threshold
  • Tax software: All major programs (TurboTax, H&R Block, TaxAct) support e-filing Form 4868
  • Mail: Paper Form 4868 must be postmarked by April 15
  • Tax professional: Your CPA or enrolled agent can file on your behalf

If you make a payment toward your estimated tax liability using IRS Direct Pay or EFTPS and designate it as an extension payment, the IRS will automatically grant an extension — no separate Form 4868 needed.

Estimating What You Owe

Part 2 of Form 4868 asks for your estimated tax liability and the amount you are paying. You do not need to be exact, but underpaying carries interest and a late payment penalty:

  • Late payment penalty: 0.5% of unpaid tax per month (up to 25%)
  • Interest: Federal short-term rate + 3%, compounded daily (roughly 8% in 2024)

To estimate, gather your W-2s, 1099s, and prior-year return as a baseline. Even a rough figure is better than paying nothing.

Good Reasons to File an Extension

  • Waiting on a corrected or late K-1, 1099, or W-2
  • Major life event: divorce, death in family, natural disaster
  • Complex situation: foreign income, multiple business entities, stock options
  • Starting with a new tax professional who needs more time
  • Self-employed with multiple income streams still being reconciled

Common Myths Debunked

Myth: An extension increases your audit risk.
Reality: The IRS has never published data supporting this. Extensions are extremely common and do not flag your return for scrutiny.

Myth: You need a reason to file an extension.
Reality: No explanation is required. The IRS grants extensions automatically to anyone who files Form 4868 by the deadline.

Myth: Filing late with an extension is the same as filing late without one.
Reality: Failing to file by the original deadline without an extension triggers a 5% per month failure-to-file penalty — 10× worse than the failure-to-pay penalty.

State Extensions

Many states automatically grant a state extension if you have a federal extension, but not all. Check your state's revenue department. States that require a separate extension request include California, New York, and Illinois.

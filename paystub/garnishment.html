Wage Garnishment: How It Works & Federal Limits (2024)

📅 Updated 2024⏱ 5 min read

Wage garnishment is a legal process that requires your employer to withhold a portion of your paycheck and send it directly to a creditor or government agency. Federal law (the Consumer Credit Protection Act / CCPA) limits how much can be garnished and protects you from being fired for a single garnishment.

Federal Garnishment Limits (CCPA)

The CCPA sets maximum garnishment limits based on disposable earnings—what's left after legally required deductions (taxes, SS, Medicare, state unemployment insurance):

Type of GarnishmentMaximum Per Pay Period
Consumer debt (credit cards, medical, personal loans)Lesser of: 25% of disposable earnings, OR amount by which disposable earnings exceed 30× federal minimum wage ($217.50/week)
Child support / alimony (supporting other family)Up to 50% of disposable earnings
Child support / alimony (not supporting other family)Up to 60% of disposable earnings
Child support / alimony (12+ weeks in arrears)Add 5% to above limits (55% or 65%)
Federal student loans (Dept. of Education)15% of disposable earnings
Federal tax levy (IRS)Based on filing status and dependents; remainder of exempt amount
Bankruptcy court orderSet by court order

How Garnishment Works

  1. Creditor gets a court judgment — after winning a lawsuit against you (except IRS and student loans, which don't need court orders)
  2. Garnishment order sent to employer — your employer is legally obligated to comply
  3. Employer withholds the amount — appears as a deduction on your pay stub (post-tax)
  4. Employer remits to creditor/agency — employer sends the withheld amount on each paycheck
  5. Continues until debt paid — or court order is satisfied

Your Rights Under Federal Law

  • Cannot be fired for one garnishment — CCPA prohibits firing for a single garnishment; firing for multiple garnishments is not prohibited federally (some states protect more)
  • Notice required — for most consumer debts you must receive notice and opportunity to respond before garnishment begins
  • Exemptions — Social Security, disability benefits, veterans benefits, and other federal benefits are generally exempt from garnishment for consumer debts
  • State laws may be stricter — many states offer additional protections or lower limits

How to Stop or Reduce Garnishment

  • Pay the debt in full
  • Negotiate a settlement — creditors often accept less than the full amount
  • File for bankruptcy — automatically stays most garnishments (not child support/alimony)
  • Claim an exemption — if your income falls below the minimum threshold
  • Challenge the judgment — if the original debt wasn't valid or there were procedural errors
⚠️
Garnishment appears on your pay stub as a post-tax deduction

Unlike 401(k) or health insurance, garnishments are deducted after taxes—from your disposable (after-tax) earnings. You still owe income tax on the full amount, even the portion garnished.

Pay Stub Deductions Explained: Pre-Tax vs. Post-Tax

📅 Updated 2024⏱ 5 min read

Deductions on your pay stub fall into two categories: pre-tax (taken before income taxes are calculated, reducing your taxable income) and post-tax (taken after taxes, no tax benefit). Maximizing pre-tax deductions like 401(k) and HSA contributions is one of the most powerful ways to increase your effective take-home pay.

Pre-Tax Deductions

Pre-tax deductions lower your taxable gross income before federal and state income taxes are calculated. This means you pay less in income tax.

Real savings example: Pre-tax 401(k) contribution

If you're in the 22% tax bracket and contribute $500/month to a traditional 401(k), you save $110/month in federal taxes alone. That $500 contribution only reduces your take-home pay by $390 — not the full $500.

Post-Tax Deductions

Post-tax deductions are taken from your paycheck after all taxes have been calculated. They don't reduce your taxable income.

DeductionExampleNotes

Wage garnishmentsChild support, student loans, court ordersLegally required, no tax benefit
Union duesMonthly union membership feesMay be deductible on Schedule A
Charitable contributionsEmployer workplace giving programsMay be deductible if you itemize

Mandatory Deductions (Not Optional)

These appear on every paycheck regardless of elections:

  • Federal Income Tax: Based on W-4 and income level
  • State Income Tax: In states that have it (not TX, FL, WA, NV, AK, WY, SD, TN, NH)
  • Social Security: 6.2% on wages up to $168,600 (2024)
  • Medicare: 1.45% on all wages; additional 0.9% over $200,000
  • Local/City Tax: Where applicable (NYC, Philadelphia, etc.)

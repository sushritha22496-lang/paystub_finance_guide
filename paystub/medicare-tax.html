Medicare Tax: 1.45% Rate & Additional 0.9% for High Earners (2024)

📅 Updated 2024⏱ 4 min read

Medicare tax (labeled Med or Medicare on your pay stub) is 1.45% on all wages—unlike Social Security, there is no wage cap. Your employer matches the 1.45%. High earners pay an additional 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on wages over $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly).

The Additional 0.9% Medicare Tax

The Additional Medicare Tax was introduced by the ACA and applies to high earners. Key rules:

  • Threshold by filing status: $200,000 (single/HoH/MFS) or $250,000 (married filing jointly)
  • Employer withholds the extra 0.9% once your wages from that employer exceed $200,000—regardless of your filing status or other income
  • Reconciled at filing: If you file MFJ with combined wages under $250,000, you get credit for over-withheld Additional Medicare Tax. If under-withheld (e.g., each spouse earns $150,000), you owe it at filing.
  • Covers wages, self-employment income, and RRTA compensation — investment income is covered by the separate 3.8% NIIT

Real-World Example

You earn $220,000 in 2024 (single filer):

  • Regular Medicare: $220,000 × 1.45% = $3,190
  • Additional Medicare: ($220,000 − $200,000) × 0.9% = $20,000 × 0.9% = $180
  • Total employee Medicare tax: $3,370
  • Employer pays additional 1.45% on full $220,000 = $3,190 (no employer match on the 0.9%)

What Medicare Tax Funds

Medicare tax funds Medicare Part A (Hospital Insurance)—inpatient hospital care, skilled nursing facility care, hospice, and some home health services. Most people don't pay a premium for Part A because they (or a spouse) paid Medicare taxes for at least 10 years (40 quarters).

💡
No wage base cap—ever

Unlike Social Security tax, Medicare tax never stops. Whether you earn $50,000 or $5,000,000, every dollar of wages is subject to the 1.45% Medicare tax. This is why Medicare tax continues to appear on your pay stub throughout the year even after Social Security withholding stops.

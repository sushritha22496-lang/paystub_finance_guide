Employee Stock Options: ISO vs NSO Explained (2024)

📅 Updated 2024⏱ 7 min read

Employee stock options give you the right to buy company stock at a preset price (the strike price). There are two main types: Incentive Stock Options (ISOs)—which get preferential capital gains tax treatment but may trigger AMT—and Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs/NQSOs)—which are taxed as ordinary income at exercise but simpler.

How NSOs Are Taxed

NSOs are straightforward—you pay ordinary income tax on the spread (difference between FMV and strike price) at exercise:

  1. Grant: No tax
  2. Exercise: Spread = (FMV at exercise − strike price) × shares. This appears as ordinary wages on your W-2 and is subject to federal/state income tax + FICA.
  3. Sale: Capital gain/loss on (sale price − FMV at exercise). Long-term rates if held 1+ year after exercise.

How ISOs Are Taxed (and AMT)

ISOs have better tax treatment but require careful planning:

  1. Grant: No tax
  2. Exercise: No regular income tax, but the spread is an AMT preference item—meaning it may trigger Alternative Minimum Tax
  3. Qualifying sale (held 2 yrs from grant + 1 yr from exercise): Entire gain taxed at long-term capital gains rates
  4. Disqualifying disposition (sold too early): Treated like NSO; spread at exercise becomes ordinary income

RSUs vs Stock Options

Restricted Stock Units (RSUs) are different from options: you receive actual shares (not the right to buy), taxed as ordinary income at vesting based on the full fair market value. No strike price, no exercise decision—simpler but no ability to benefit from growth before vesting.

⚠️
Exercise timing is a major tax decision

For ISOs especially, when you exercise dramatically affects your tax bill. Exercising early (when the spread is small) minimizes AMT exposure. Consult a CPA or financial advisor before exercising significant stock options—a poorly timed exercise can result in a large AMT bill even if the stock later drops.

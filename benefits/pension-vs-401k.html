Pension vs. 401(k): Key Differences Explained

Updated June 2026 • 10 min read
Quick Answer: A pension (defined benefit) guarantees a monthly income in retirement based on your salary and years of service. A 401(k) (defined contribution) depends on how much you save and how your investments perform — you bear the investment risk.

The Core Difference: Who Bears the Risk

The fundamental distinction between a pension and a 401(k) is who bears the investment risk:

  • Pension: The employer promises a fixed monthly payment for life. If investments underperform, the employer must make up the shortfall. You receive a predictable amount regardless of market conditions.
  • 401(k): You and your employer contribute to an account. The balance grows based on market performance. In retirement, you draw from your own balance — which could be smaller or larger than expected.

How Pension Benefits Are Calculated

Most public and private pension plans use a formula like:

Monthly benefit = Years of service × Average salary × Benefit multiplier

Example: 30 years of service, final 3-year average salary of $80,000, multiplier of 2%:
30 × $80,000 × 2% = $48,000/year ($4,000/month)

Multipliers typically range from 1.5% to 2.5%. Some plans use career-average salary instead of final salary, which generally produces a lower benefit.

Vesting: When the Benefit Becomes Yours

With pensions, vesting means your right to the earned benefit is permanent — even if you leave the employer. Common vesting schedules:

  • Cliff vesting: 0% until year 5, then 100%
  • Graded vesting: 20% per year from years 3–7
  • Immediate vesting: Common with some government plans

For 401(k) employer matches, ERISA sets similar vesting requirements. Contributions you make yourself are always 100% vested immediately.

If You Have Both

Many government and union employees have both a pension and access to a supplemental savings plan (457, 403b, or 401k). Strategy recommendations:

  • Max out the supplemental plan since your pension provides a guaranteed base
  • Do not reduce pension contributions to fund the 401k — you forfeit years-of-service credit
  • Model both income streams at different claiming ages to optimize total retirement income

The Decline of Pensions

In 1980, about 60% of private-sector workers had a pension. Today, fewer than 4% do. The shift to 401(k) plans transferred investment risk from employers to employees. Government and military workers remain the primary recipients of traditional pensions.

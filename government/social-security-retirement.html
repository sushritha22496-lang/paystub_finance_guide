Social Security Retirement Benefits: How Much Will You Get? (2024)

📅 Updated 2024⏱ 7 min read

Social Security retirement benefits are calculated from your 35 highest-earning years, adjusted for inflation. In 2024, the average monthly retirement benefit is $1,907, and the maximum is $3,822 (at full retirement age). When you claim—as early as 62 or as late as 70—significantly affects your monthly amount.

Claiming Age Impact

Claiming AgeEffect on BenefitExample ($1,500 FRA benefit)
62 (earliest)Reduced by up to 30%~$1,050/month
65Reduced by ~13%~$1,305/month
67 (Full Retirement Age, born 1960+)100% of benefit$1,500/month
70 (maximum)Increased by 24% (8%/yr × 3 yrs)$1,860/month

Full Retirement Age (FRA) is 66 for those born 1943–1954, gradually rising to 67 for those born 1960 or later.

How Benefits Are Calculated

  1. Find your 35 highest earning years (indexed to current dollars)
  2. Calculate AIME (Average Indexed Monthly Earnings): sum of those earnings ÷ 420 months
  3. Apply the PIA formula (Primary Insurance Amount, 2024 bend points):
    • 90% of AIME up to $1,174
    • 32% of AIME from $1,174 to $7,078
    • 15% of AIME over $7,078
  4. Adjust for claiming age

Spousal and Survivor Benefits

  • Spousal benefit: Up to 50% of spouse's FRA benefit (if higher than your own); must be 62+ to claim
  • Divorced spouse: Entitled to spousal benefit if married 10+ years, currently unmarried
  • Survivor benefit: 100% of deceased spouse's benefit; can claim as early as age 60 (50 if disabled)
  • Delayed credits don't apply to spousal benefits—only your own record benefits from waiting past FRA

Taxation of Social Security Benefits

Up to 85% of Social Security benefits may be taxable depending on your "combined income" (AGI + nontaxable interest + half of SS benefits):

  • Under $25,000 single / $32,000 MFJ: 0% of benefits taxable
  • $25,000–$34,000 single / $32,000–$44,000 MFJ: Up to 50% taxable
  • Over $34,000 single / $44,000 MFJ: Up to 85% taxable
💡
Break-even age for delaying to 70

Claiming at 70 vs 67 means ~3 years of no payments to get a 24% higher check. The break-even point is typically around age 82–83. If you expect to live longer than that (and don't need the income), delaying generally pays off. If health is a concern, claiming earlier may be better.

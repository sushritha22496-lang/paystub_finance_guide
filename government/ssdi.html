What Is SSDI? Social Security Disability Benefits Explained (2024)

📅 Updated 2024⏱ 6 min read

SSDI (Social Security Disability Insurance) is a federal program that pays monthly benefits to workers who become disabled and can no longer work. Unlike SSI, SSDI is based on your work history and Social Security taxes paid—not financial need. In 2024, the average SSDI benefit is about $1,537/month, with a maximum of $3,822/month.

SSDI Eligibility: Work Credits

To qualify for SSDI, you must have worked long enough and recently enough to have accumulated sufficient work credits. In 2024, you earn 1 credit per $1,730 in wages, up to 4 credits per year.

The 5-Step Disability Evaluation

SSA uses a sequential 5-step process to determine if you're disabled:

  1. Are you working? If earning above Substantial Gainful Activity ($1,550/month in 2024 for non-blind), you're not disabled.
  2. Is your condition severe? Must significantly limit basic work activities for 12+ months or result in death.
  3. Is it on the Listing of Impairments? If it meets SSA's "Blue Book" criteria, you're automatically approved.
  4. Can you do your past work? If yes, not disabled.
  5. Can you do any other work? Considering age, education, and work experience—if yes, not disabled.

SSDI Benefit Amounts (2024)

SSDI payments are based on your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME)—your lifetime Social Security-taxed earnings, indexed for inflation:

  • Average monthly benefit: ~$1,537
  • Maximum monthly benefit: $3,822 (for high earners with long work history)
  • 5-month waiting period: Benefits don't start until your 6th month of disability
  • Back pay: SSA pays retroactive benefits up to 12 months before application date (minus the 5-month wait)

Medicare After 24 Months

SSDI recipients automatically become eligible for Medicare after receiving disability benefits for 24 months. This applies regardless of age—even if you're under 65. The 24-month clock starts from the first month of entitlement (month 6 after onset), not the application date.

💡
SSDI vs SSI: Key Difference

SSDI is for workers with a disability who have paid Social Security taxes. SSI is for disabled individuals with very low income and assets, regardless of work history. You can potentially receive both if your SSDI benefit is very low. See our SSI guide for details.
